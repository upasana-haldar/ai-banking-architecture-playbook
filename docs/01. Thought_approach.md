# Example : Your bank wants to automate fraud detection for card transactions in real-time using AI.
### 👉 Design a high-level solution architecture

#### From a **business perspective**, 
the objective is to reduce fraud loss, respond faster to suspicious transactions, protect customer trust, and maintain regulatory compliance. In banking, fraud detection is not only about stopping bad transactions, but also about minimizing false positives so genuine customers are not unnecessarily impacted.

#### From a **data flow perspective**, 
transaction events would originate from channels such as card switches, payment systems, or fraud reporting queues. These events would flow into a streaming or queue-based ingestion layer. The system should enrich the transaction with customer profile, account history, device or channel context, velocity checks, and past fraud indicators before decisioning.

#### From an **architecture layer perspective**, 
I would break the solution into five layers.
1. First, the **ingestion layer** to capture transaction or fraud alert events from core banking, card systems, or partner channels.
2. Second, the i**ntegration and enrichment** layer using APIs and middleware to fetch additional customer and account context.
3. Third, the **decision layer**, where both AI models and deterministic rules operate together.
4. Fourth, the **orchestration and action layer**, where an automation platform triggers actions such as placing a lien, restricting the account, updating CRM, routing to Unity or Etihad Portal, and notifying operations teams.
5. Fifth, the **monitoring and audit layer**, where every decision, action, and exception is logged for reporting, compliance, and investigation.

#### For the **decision engine**, 
I would not rely only on AI. **In banking, the best design is usually a hybrid model**.

Rules handle clear regulatory and policy-driven scenarios, such as known **fraud patterns, threshold breaches, or mandatory restriction logic**.
AI handles **pattern-based anomaly detection**, **transaction risk scoring**, and i**dentification of suspicious behavior** that static rules may miss.
**The final decision should be policy-driven**: for example, 
1. **high-risk scores** may auto-block,
2. **medium-risk scores** may create a review case, and
3. **low-risk scores** may allow the transaction with passive monitoring.

#### For **integration**, 
APIs should connect to core banking, CRM, case management tools, fraud systems, and operational portals such as Unity and Etihad. 
The automation layer **should not** contain all the business logic. Instead, **orchestration should call modular services** so the **design remains scalable and maintainable**.

### From a **risk and compliance lens**, 
I would include strong controls:

#### Full **audit trail** 
1. of why a **transaction was blocked or restricted**
2. **Role-based access** for manual overrides
3. **Maker-checker control** for sensitive actions where needed
4. **Data masking** for sensitive customer fields
5. **Model explainability** for AI-supported decisions
6. Clear **distinction between automated decision, assisted decision, and manual review**
7. **Exception handling and fallback** if any **downstream system is unavailable**

#### For **monitoring**, 
I would track both **technical and business KPIs**.
1. **Technical metric**s include **API latency**, **queue backlog**, **automation failures**, and **system availability**.
2. **Business metrics** include **fraud prevented**, **false positive rate**, **number of liens placed**, **turnaround time**, **manual review volume**, and **customer impact**.
     This can feed into Metabase dashboards, while operational failures or exceptions can raise tickets into Freshworks or another incident workflow.

Overall, the architecture should be **event-driven**, **auditable**, **hybrid between AI and rules**, and designed so that fraud control does not come at the cost of **customer experience**.
